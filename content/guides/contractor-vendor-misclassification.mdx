Software and game workplaces often mix direct employees, staffing-firm workers, vendors, outsourced QA, and "independent contractors" doing tightly managed work. That matters because workers who sit side by side can still be in different legal and strategic lanes.

## Why this needs its own lane

When classification is messy, workers can make avoidable mistakes very quickly. A plan that makes sense for one group may expose another group if nobody has slowed down to ask who technically employs whom, who controls the work, and how the workplace is segmented on purpose.

That does not mean workers should freeze. It means the structure question matters early.

## Signs classification may matter

- you work under someone else's company name but take daily direction from the client
- your badge, access, or email status is used to keep you separate from "real employees"
- your title says contractor, but the schedule and supervision look like regular employment
- entire teams are segmented by vendor or staffing channel
- workers doing the same job seem to be in different formal categories

## What to compare safely

- who sets your schedule and priorities
- who reviews your work day to day
- which company handles pay, discipline, and termination paperwork
- whether similar workers have different classifications
- whether separation is being used to weaken information flow or leverage

## Why this changes organizing

Classification questions do not make collective action impossible. They change how careful workers need to be about scope, sequencing, and who should be visible when.

A vendor team, a mixed team, and a direct-employee team may all need different tactical plans even when the underlying grievance is shared.

## Work-mode considerations

### In person

Segregation often shows up in access, seating, meetings, badges, and who gets included in casual information flow.

### Hybrid

Workers can look integrated online while still being formally split by payroll chain or vendor status.

### Remote

Distributed vendor teams can be isolated by time zone, tooling, and manager structure, which makes mapping more important.

## What not to do

Do not guess at legal status. Do not tell workers to ignore formal boundaries they do not understand. Do not put contractor coworkers in a highly visible role before the structure is clearer.

## When to get help early

Move carefully and get outside advice early if the workplace is split across vendors, staffing firms, or contractor categories, or if classification seems to be part of how management keeps workers fragmented.
